# 04 — EXPERIENCE COMPOSITION

## Composing Meaning Across Time

**Creative Principles define how creation grows.**

**Creative Phenomena make that growth perceptible.**

**Experience Composition determines how those expressions become one experience.**

## 04.0 — PURPOSE

This chapter defines how Photography,
Typography,
Copy,
Space,
Interface,
and Creative Phenomena
are composed around one coherent experience.

An experience is not a collection of elements.

It is the way meaning appears,
becomes understandable,
and continues into what follows.

Experience Composition does not begin by asking where an element should be placed.

It begins by asking:

- What should be perceived here?
- What should remain after this Moment?
- Which expression carries that meaning best?
- What should stay quiet?
- What should continue into the next Moment?

**Compose the experience before composing the screen.**

### COMPOSITION BEFORE ARRANGEMENT

Arrangement asks where an element belongs.

Composition asks what role it performs.

A photograph may establish trust,
make a place tangible,
or give language a physical reference.

A Creative Phenomenon may reveal emergence,
connect two Moments,
or make a change perceptible.

Neither should be used only because space is available
or because the expression is visually attractive.

Form follows role.

Role follows the meaning of the Moment.

### CHAPTER BOUNDARY

01 — MANIFESTO defines why the Creative System exists
and what it seeks to express.

02 — CREATIVE PRINCIPLES defines how an 音のタネ grows.

03 — CREATIVE PHENOMENA defines how that growth becomes perceptible.

04 — EXPERIENCE COMPOSITION defines how principles,
phenomena,
photography,
language,
and interface become one experience.

This chapter sits between philosophy and implementation.

It does not define:

- UI component specifications.
- form or reservation workflows.
- loading, error, or processing states.
- responsive layout values.
- animation duration or easing.
- technical implementation.
- conversion patterns.

Those decisions belong to
the future Interaction Extension
and 07 — IMPLEMENTATION CONTRACT.

The primary context of this chapter is expressive and editorial experience:

- brand sites.
- studio and environment presentation.
- project and work archives.
- service and creative philosophy pages.
- experiences composed through Photography, language, Space, and Creative Phenomena.

### CORE TERMINOLOGY

**Moment**

One central change in perception.

**Dominant Moment**

The Moment that currently holds
the center of the perceptual frame.

**Primary**

The element or relationship
that directly establishes the change
within one Moment.

**Supporting**

An element or expression
that clarifies or deepens
the Primary meaning.

**Focus**

The Primary meaning
toward which attention moves.

**Pattern**

A reusable relationship
between one or more Moments,
defined in 06 — COMPOSITION PATTERNS.

A Pattern does not replace
the individual perceptual change
defined for each Moment.

### PHASE AND PATTERN NAMING

Lowercase terms such as:

```text
entry
statement
movement
release
```

describe phases within attention,
rhythm,
or temporal behavior.

Capitalized terms such as:

```text
Entry
Statement
Release
```

refer to Core Composition Patterns
defined in 06 — COMPOSITION PATTERNS.

Release Principle refers
to the Creative Principle defined in 02.

Release Pattern refers
to the Composition Pattern defined in 06.

## 04.1 — ONE MOMENT PER SCREEN

A Screen may contain many elements,
but it should hold one primary Moment.

A Moment is not a component,
a route,
or a single action.

It is one central change in perception.

Examples include:

- discovering that a place is real.
- sensing the atmosphere of creation.
- understanding the origin of a work.
- imagining oneself within a creative process.
- receiving the meaning of a completed piece.
- becoming ready to continue.

Multiple elements may belong to one Moment
when they support the same change.

### SCREEN AS A PERCEPTUAL FRAME

Screen does not always mean a complete page.

It means the range a person perceives as one meaningful unit.

A Screen may be:

- the first viewport.
- one section.
- a photograph and its language.
- a short interval within a scroll.
- one stable state of a composition.

One Moment per Screen is not a rule for reducing content.

It is a rule for preserving one center of meaning.

### DEFINE THE CHANGE

Before composing a Moment,
complete the following statement:

```text
Through this Moment,
the person moves from ______
to ______.
```

For example:

```text
from not knowing what kind of place this is
to trusting it as a real environment for creation.
```

```text
from seeing only a finished work
to sensing the process that allowed it to emerge.
```

```text
from reading a service description
to imagining personal creation within it.
```

This change determines what belongs in the Moment.

An element that does not support the change
should be reduced,
moved,
or removed.

### PRIMARY, SUPPORTING, QUIET

Elements within a Moment have three roles.

**Primary**

The element or relationship that directly establishes the Moment.

**Supporting**

The information or expression that clarifies or deepens the Primary meaning.

**Quiet**

An element that may remain present
but does not ask for attention at this time.

Not every element should become Primary.

The goal is not to remove every secondary element.

The goal is to prevent multiple centers from competing.

### ONE MOMENT, MULTIPLE MEDIA

Photography carries the reality of trust.

Creative Phenomena carry the reality of creation.

Copy connects perception to meaning.

Typography shapes emphasis and reading speed.

Space creates the time needed to receive them.

Interface reveals the possibility of continuing.

These media may work together,
but they must not begin separate stories within the same Moment.

**A Screen may have many voices,
but they must speak toward one meaning.**

### VISUAL CENTER AND EXPERIENTIAL CENTER

The largest element is not automatically the center of the experience.

A large photograph may remain Supporting
while a short sentence carries the Primary meaning.

A visually dominant image without a clear role
may attract attention without creating a Moment.

Visual dominance should support experiential dominance
or remain intentionally secondary.

### THE BOUNDARY OF A MOMENT

A Moment must end before another becomes dominant.

A section should not simultaneously attempt to:

- establish the reality of a place.
- explain creative philosophy.
- compare services.
- prove results.
- create urgency.
- begin a different story.

The next Moment may be suggested.

It should not fully begin before the current meaning has settled.

### MOBILE AS THE TEST

Mobile reveals the true order of the experience.

Desktop can display several relationships at once.

Mobile reveals them over time,
one viewport after another.

Each Moment should therefore be tested for:

- a clear center within the first relevant viewport.
- a stable relationship between Photography and Copy.
- Supporting content that does not become a second protagonist.
- a perceptible ending.
- a transition that does not begin too early.

Mobile First is not the reduction of a large composition.

It is the verification of meaning at its most immediate scale.

## 04.2 — ATTENTION AND RHYTHM

Attention defines where meaning begins.

Rhythm defines how long meaning is given to unfold.

They cannot be separated.

Spatial hierarchy becomes perceptual sequence.

Silence,
statement,
movement,
and release determine how the sequence is felt.

**Attention gives meaning direction.**

**Rhythm gives meaning time.**

### ANCHOR AND FOCUS

Each Moment needs an Attention Anchor.

The Anchor answers:

**What is happening here?**

It may be a photograph,
a heading,
a sentence,
a state,
or a Creative Phenomenon.

The Focus is the Primary meaning toward which attention moves.

Anchor and Focus may be the same element.

They may also occur in sequence,
with the Anchor opening the Moment
and the Focus becoming clear afterward.

A person should not need to compare several strong elements
in order to discover the center.

### ATTENTION PATH

A useful perceptual sequence is:

```text
entry
→ focus
→ support
→ release
```

**entry**

Signals that a new Moment has begun.

**Focus**

Establishes the central meaning.

**Support**

Provides the context needed to understand or deepen it.

**release**

Reduces intensity,
allows meaning to settle,
and prepares for what follows.

This sequence does not force one exact reading path.

It creates a structure in which different reading behaviors
can still preserve the same center.

### ATTENTION THROUGH DIFFERENCE

Attention is created through difference:

- scale and restraint.
- density and Space.
- movement and stillness.
- contrast and quiet.
- long language and short language.
- Primary and Supporting presence.

If everything is large,
nothing is clearly large.

If everything moves,
movement no longer indicates change.

If every section is expressive,
expression no longer creates emphasis.

**Attention is created by contrast in role,
not by emphasis in isolation.**

### ATTENTION BUDGET

A Moment has limited capacity for strong expression.

The following all carry a high Attention Cost:

- large Typography.
- strong color.
- prominent Photography.
- active Creative Phenomena.
- fixed Interface.
- high information density.
- sudden change.
- strong contrast.

They should not all become Primary at once.

When Creative Phenomena are strong,
Photography or Typography may need to become quieter.

When Photography is Primary,
Creative Phenomena may remain only as a trace.

Attention Budget is not a restriction on expression.

It preserves enough strength for the meaning that truly requires it.

### RHYTHM OF EXPERIENCE

A reference rhythm for USDL is:

```text
silence
→ statement
→ movement
→ release
```

**Silence**

Creates room before meaning appears.

**statement**

Makes the center of the Moment clear.

**Movement**

Allows relation,
change,
or progression to become perceptible.

**release**

Reduces intensity
and allows the experience to become stable again.

This is not a universal animation sequence.

It is a compositional reference for deciding
how a Moment begins,
holds attention,
and ends.

### STILLNESS IS ACTIVE

Stillness is not the absence of experience.

It gives a person time to:

- observe a photograph.
- receive a sentence.
- understand a relationship.
- hold the trace of a phenomenon.
- prepare for the next Moment.

**Movement makes change perceptible.**

**Stillness makes meaning stable.**

Creative Phenomena should not remain active
only to keep a page visually alive.

When their role is complete,
they should settle,
leave,
or become quiet enough for meaning to remain.

### PAGE RHYTHM

A page should not repeat the same intensity
from beginning to end.

Strong Moments require quieter Moments around them.

A page may move between:

- expressive Moments.
- informational Moments.
- photographic Moments.
- reflective Moments.
- brief transitional Moments.

If every section behaves like a climax,
no section can hold one.

The page should be composed more like music
than like a sequence of promotional panels.

## 04.3 — CONTINUITY AND RESTRAINT

A page is not a collection of isolated sections.

It becomes one experience
when meaning carries forward from one Moment to the next.

Continuity preserves the thread.

Restraint protects it from excess.

**Continuity connects meaning.**

**Restraint protects meaning.**

### CARRY MEANING FORWARD

The next Moment should not begin as though nothing came before it.

If a previous Moment established:

- the reality of a place.
- the possibility of creation.
- the origin of a work.
- the presence of trust.
- the feeling of emergence.

then the next Moment should build from that understanding.

Continuity is not repetition.

The same explanation does not need to appear again.

The next Moment should preserve what has already become clear
and add one new layer of meaning.

### PRESERVE THE THREAD, CHANGE THE FORM

A meaning may continue through different media.

A Creative Phenomenon may first express expansion.

The next Moment may reveal that expansion through physical space in Photography.

A sentence may introduce a belief.

A later project may make that belief concrete.

A trace may begin as motion
and remain as still composition.

**Preserve the meaning,
not necessarily the form.**

Continuity does not require visual repetition.

It requires perceptible relationship.

### TRANSITIONS SHOULD HAVE CAUSE

A transition should reveal why the next Moment follows.

Examples include:

- creative philosophy leading to actual work.
- a real environment leading to what happens within it.
- a finished work leading to the process behind it.
- an abstract phenomenon resolving into Photography.
- a question leading to evidence.

Creative Phenomena may support a transition
when they carry the change in meaning.

They should not be used only as decorative scene changes.

### ROLE SEPARATION

Photography,
Copy,
Interface,
and Creative Phenomena
should not all perform the same role.

**Photography**

Shows what is there
and carries the reality of trust.

**Creative Phenomena**

Reveal what is changing
and carry the reality of creation.

**Copy**

Connects perception to meaning
without explaining everything twice.

**Interface**

Reveals how the experience may continue
without becoming the protagonist of the Moment.

Repeatedly expressing the same meaning through every medium
does not make the experience deeper.

Depth comes from distinct roles working together.

### EXPRESSION MUST HAVE A ROLE

Every prominent expression should be explainable in one sentence.

For example:

- This photograph establishes the reality of the place.
- This phenomenon reveals that separate presences are beginning to resonate.
- This Space preserves the trace of the previous Moment.
- This sentence gives a way to understand the work that follows.
- This Interface provides a quiet path toward more detail.

The following are not sufficient reasons to add expression:

- the Screen feels empty.
- the page needs more motion.
- the composition should look more designed.
- another section uses the same device.
- the technology is available.
- AI generated an interesting result.

**A possible expression is not automatically a necessary expression.**

### DO NOT MAKE EVERY MOMENT EXPRESSIVE

Not every Moment requires Creative Phenomena.

Not every section requires a large photograph.

Not every heading needs to become a statement.

A Moment may be completed by:

- Photography alone.
- Copy and Space.
- a quiet compositional shift.
- a small Interface cue.
- stillness.

The absence of a phenomenon may be the correct expression.

**Restraint is not the absence of expression.**

**It is the selection of expression.**

### AI AND RESTRAINT

AI can generate variations,
combinations,
transitions,
and phenomena quickly.

The quantity it can generate
must not determine the quantity the experience uses.

AI may expand possibility.

Human judgment decides:

- what belongs to the Moment.
- what remains true to creation.
- what is excessive.
- what supports the brand.
- what should be removed.

**AI may expand possibility.**

**Human judgment gives it form.**

### AVOID REPEATED CLIMAXES

Strong expression should not be repeated without release.

A large Hero,
active Phenomena,
full-screen Photography,
large Typography,
and another visual climax
should not be placed in continuous competition.

A strong Moment should be followed by space for:

- evidence.
- understanding.
- reflection.
- transition.
- quiet.

A page should not make every section its chorus.

### END WITH RELEASE

The end of an experience should not introduce an unrelated final climax.

It should allow a person to retain:

- what was seen.
- what was felt.
- what became trustworthy.
- what may happen next.

A final Interface may be present.

It should emerge as a natural continuation of the experience,
not as a sudden change in tone.

A release phase is not an empty ending.

It is the point at which the experience reaches an appropriate level of quiet.

## CHAPTER DECISION RULES

The following rules are normative.

Codex and other implementation agents should interpret
**MUST** as required,
**SHOULD** as the default unless a documented reason exists,
and **MAY** as optional.

### MUST

1. Each perceptual frame MUST have one Dominant Moment.
2. Every prominent element MUST support that Moment or remain intentionally secondary.
3. Photography, Copy, Interface, and Creative Phenomena MUST have distinguishable roles.
4. Strong expressions MUST NOT compete for equal priority within one Moment.
5. Creative Phenomena MUST represent meaning, relation, or change.
6. A Moment MUST become stable before another Moment becomes dominant.
7. Mobile MUST be used to verify the perceptual order of the experience.
8. An expression without a definable role MUST be removed.

### SHOULD

1. Each Moment SHOULD be describable as a change in perception.
2. Attention SHOULD move from Anchor to Focus, then toward Supporting meaning.
3. Strong Moments SHOULD be followed by quieter Moments.
4. Meaning SHOULD carry forward without repeating the same explanation.
5. Adjacent Moments SHOULD preserve a perceptible relationship.
6. Creative Phenomena SHOULD return to stillness after their role is complete.
7. The end of a page SHOULD provide release rather than introduce an unrelated climax.

### MAY

1. A Moment MAY include multiple elements and forms of media.
2. A page MAY contain several Moments.
3. A Moment MAY span more than one viewport when its center remains clear.
4. Creative Phenomena MAY be absent when Photography, Copy, Space, or Interface already completes the Moment.
5. The same meaning MAY continue through different forms across adjacent Moments.

### CODEX INTERPRETATION BOUNDARY

Codex MUST NOT infer that:

- every section requires animation.
- every section requires a Creative Phenomenon.
- every Screen requires a call to action.
- every Moment corresponds to a route.
- every Mobile viewport contains only one component.
- visual consistency requires repeating the same composition.
- more generated variation creates a better experience.
- decorative motion can substitute for meaning.
- all available content must be displayed.
- brand expression may override legibility.

When a design decision is unclear,
use the following order of priority:

1. Preserve the Dominant Moment.
2. Preserve legibility and meaning.
3. Preserve continuity with adjacent Moments.
4. Preserve the distinct role of each medium.
5. Reduce competing expression.
6. Remove elements before adding emphasis.

## DEFERRED PRINCIPLES

The following principles remain valid,
but they primarily concern interactive products
rather than expressive or editorial experiences.

They are reserved for the future Interaction Extension:

```text
Interaction Extension
├── Interaction Principles
├── Workflow Patterns
└── Interface States
```

### SHOW BEFORE ASKING

Core principle:

**Show the state,
possibility,
and consequence
before asking for action.**

Primary contexts include:

- forms.
- sign-up and login.
- reservations.
- plan selection.
- consent.
- confirmation.
- irreversible actions.
- AI input experiences.

This principle should define what a person needs to understand
before being asked to input,
select,
confirm,
or commit.

It is not part of Experience Composition
unless a Moment contains a meaningful interactive decision.

### STATE AS EXPERIENCE

Core principle:

**Do not add status to the Interface.**

**Let the Interface become the state.**

Primary contexts include:

- initial.
- available.
- selected.
- processing.
- pending.
- complete.
- empty.
- error.
- restricted.
- unavailable.

This principle should define how appearance,
time,
and available behavior change together.

It is especially relevant to reservation systems,
account pages,
dashboards,
and administrative interfaces.

For expressive and editorial pages,
the higher-level principle remains:

**The composition should reflect the meaning of the current Moment.**

For interactive products,
the more specific principle remains:

**The Interface should become the state it represents.**
